Acantholipan
Acantholipan is a [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus"] genus of herbivorous [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nodosauridae"] nodosaurid [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur"] dinosaur
 from Mexico from the early Santonian age of the Late
Cretaceous. It includes one species, Acantholipan gonzalezi.[1]
Discovery and naming
In the north of Mexico, fragmentary fossils have been found of
nodosaurids. A partial skeleton excavated at [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Los_Primos&action=edit&redlink=1"] Los Primos near
 San Miguel in Coahuila, was described in 2011. When Rivera-
Sylva and colleagues reported the discovery of this specimen,
CPC 272, they initially considered it too fragmentary to name.[2]
Later it was judged that the remains were sufficiently distinct to
be given a [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_name"] binomial name.
In 2018, the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_species"] type species Acantholipan gonzalezi was named by
 Héctor Eduardo Rivera-Sylva, Eberhard Frey, Wolfgang
 Stinnesbeck, Gerardo Carbot-Chanona, Iván Erick Sanchez-
Uribe and [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Rub%C3%A9n_Guzm%C3%A1n-Guti%C3%A1rrez&action=edit&redlink=1"] José Rubén Guzmán-Gutiárrez. The generic name
combines a Greek akanthos,  "spine",  with lipan,  the usual
Spanish designation of the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A9pai-Nd%C3%A9&action=edit&redlink=1"] Lépai-Ndé, the "Gray People", a tribe
of the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apache"] Apache inhabiting the area of the find. The [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)"] specific name
 honours the Mexican paleontologist Arturo Homero González-
González, the chairman of the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Museo_del_Desierto&action=edit&redlink=1"] Museo del Desierto at Saltillo.
Acantholipan is the first ankylosaurian species named from
Mexico.[1]

The [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holotype"] holotype specimen, CPC 272, was found in a marine layer of the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pen_Formation"] Pen Formation and dates from the
Santonian. It consists of partial skeleton lacking the skull. Its remains include a back vertebra, a tail
vertebra, a piece of a rib, the underside of the left humerus, an upper left ulna, the underside of the left
[URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femur"] femur and a spike-like [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osteoderm"] osteoderm probably from the posterior thorax.[2] The fossil is part of the Colección
Paleontológica de Coahuila, Museo del Desierto, Saltillo.[1]
