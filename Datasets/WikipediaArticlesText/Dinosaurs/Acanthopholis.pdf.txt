Acanthopholis
Acanthopholis ([URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English"] /ˌækənˈθɒfoʊlɪs/; meaning "spiny scales") is a
 genus of [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankylosaurian"] ankylosaurian [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur"] dinosaur in the family [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nodosauridae"] Nodosauridae that
lived during the Late Cretaceous [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_(geology)"] Period of England. A single
species, A. horrida, exists.[1]

Around 1865 commercial fossil collector [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Griffiths_(fossil_collector)&action=edit&redlink=1"] John Griffiths found  [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature"] Binomial name
some dinosaurian remains, including osteoderms, at the shoreline †Acanthopholis horrida
near [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folkestone"] Folkestone in Kent, which he sold to the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallurgist"] metallurgist Dr.
 John Percy. Percy brought them to the attention of Thomas 
Huxley, 1867
Henry Huxley, who paid Griffiths to dig up all fossils he could
find at the site. Despite being hampered by the fact that it was located between the tidemarks, he managed
to uncover several additional bones and parts of the body armour.
In 1867 Huxley named the genus and species Acanthopholis horridus.[1] The dinosaur's generic name
refers to its armour, being derived from [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek"] Greek άκανθα akantha meaning 'spine' or 'thorn' and φόλις pholis
 meaning 'scale'.  The specific name horridus means 'frightening' or 'rough' in Latin. Arthur Smith
Woodward emended the species name to Acanthopholis horrida in 1890 because pholis is feminine.[2]
The [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_specimen"] type specimens, [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotype"] cotypes GSM 109045-GSM 109058, were found in the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalk_Group"] Chalk Group,[3] a formation
itself dating to the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albian"] Albian to [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cenomanian"] Cenomanian [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faunal_stage"] stages around 100 million years ago. The specimens consist of
three teeth, a basicranium, a dorsal vertebra, spikes and scutes.

In 1869 [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Govier_Seeley"] Harry Govier Seeley informally named several new species of the genus based on remains from
the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_Greensand"] Cambridge Greensand: "Acanthopholis macrocercus", based on specimens CAMSM B55570-55609;
"Acanthopholis platypus" (CAMSM B55454-55461);  and "Acanthopholis stereocercus" (CAMSM
B55558-55569).[4] Seeley (1871) formally described Acanthopholis platypus as a new species, and in his
1876 description of the sauropod Macrurosaurus semnus he considered the metatarsals included in the A.
platypus type material to be possibly conspecific with Macrurosaurus.[5][6] Later, Seeley split the material
of Acanthopholis stereocercus and based a new species of [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anoplosaurus"] Anoplosaurus on part of it: Anoplosaurus major.
He also described a new species, Acanthopholis eucercus, on the basis of six caudal vertebrae (CAMSM
55552-55557).[7] In 1902 however [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Nopcsa"] Franz Nopcsa changed it into another species of Acanthopholis:
Acanthopholis major. Nopcsa at the same time renamed Anoplosaurus curtonotus into Acanthopholis
curtonotus. In 1879 Seeley named the genus Syngonosaurus based on part of the type material of "A.
macrocercus". In 1956 [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_von_Huene"] Friedrich von Huene renamed A. platypus into [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrurosaurus"] Macrurosaurus platypus.[8]
In 1999 [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Xabier_Pereda-Superbiola&action=edit&redlink=1"] Xabier Pereda-Superbiola and [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paul_M._Barrett&action=edit&redlink=1"] Paul M. Barrett reviewed all Acanthopholis material. They
concluded that all species were [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomen_dubium"] nomina dubia whose syntype specimens were composites of non-diagnostic
ankylosaur and ornithopod remains. For example, the metatarsals included in the syntype series of
Acanthopholis platypus are from a sauropod, but the remaining syntypes are not. They also found two
previously unpublished names which Seeley had used to label museum specimens: "Acanthopholis
hughesii" indicated SMC B55463-55490 and "Acanthopholis keepingi" SMC B55491-55526. Both names
were not proposed by them as new species and are [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomen_nudum"] nomina nuda.[9]
 Syngonosaurus was synonymised with Acanthopholis in 1999, but the genus was reinstated in a 2020
study, when Syngonosaurus and [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eucercosaurus"] Eucercosaurus were reinterpreted as basal iguanodontians.[10]
Description
The armour of Acanthopholis consisted of oval keeled plates set almost horizontally into the skin, with long
spikes protruding from the neck and shoulder area, along the spine. Acanthopholis was [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadruped"] quadrupedal and
herbivorous. Its size has been estimated to be in the range of 3 to 5.5 meters (10 to 18 ft) long and
approximately 380 kilograms (840 lb) in weight.
Classification
Acanthopholis was originally assigned to the [URL: "https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scelidosauridae"] Scelidosauridae by Huxley.[1] In 1902 Nopcsa created a
separate family Acanthopholididae.[11] Later, he named Acanthopholinae as a subfamily. In 1928, he
corrected Acanthopholididae to Acanthopholidae.[12] Today Acanthopholis is considered a member of the
 Nodosauridae within the Ankylosauria.
